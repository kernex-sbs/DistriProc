\begin{abstract}
Checkpoint/Restore In Userspace (CRIU) supports lazy restore, which lets a
process resume execution before all of its pages have been transferred from a
remote page server. Lazy restore dramatically reduces time-to-first-request
(TTFR) for workloads with small active footprints, but leaves the prefetch
policy unspecified: should the runtime fetch additional pages speculatively,
and how many?
This paper presents an empirical characterization of how fixed sequential
prefetch interacts with CRIU lazy restore in the post-restore remote-memory
phase, and shows that whether prefetch helps or hurts is governed by one
variable: round-trip time (RTT). At loopback RTT, fixed prefetch
\emph{increases} memory-heavy TTFR by $88\,\%$ ($650\!\to\!1{,}227$\,ms,
$n\!=\!20$) because speculative traffic congests the TCP channel shared with
fault resolution, even as it cuts the fault count by $85\,\%$, so fault
reduction is not a reliable proxy for TTFR. By injecting RTT with
\texttt{netem} we locate a crossover near $\approx\!125\,\mu$s RTT: below it
the channel is congestion-bound and prefetch is harmful; above it the
channel is latency-bound and prefetch instead \emph{reduces} TTFR (by
$37\,\%$ at $1$\,ms RTT, where demand-only lazy serializes thousands of
round trips). A real two-machine LAN run confirms the above-crossover regime
on physical hardware, where prefetch cuts TTFR by $72\,\%$ at $311\,\mu$s.
We then present \distriproc, a userspace runtime whose
feedback controller monitors duplicate-fetch pressure and queue depth and
disables prefetch under congestion. In the congestion-bound regime the
controller recovers essentially all of the loopback regression
($1{,}227\!\to\!655$\,ms; Welch $t\!=\!46.1$, $p<10^{-9}$; within $5.5$\,ms
of demand-only, $p\!=\!0.42$); above the crossover, where prefetch is
beneficial, the same congestion-driven disable behaves erratically (in one
case slower than leaving prefetch on), motivating an RTT-aware controller. The contribution is a
measurement study of \emph{when} speculative prefetch helps post-restore,
with the controller as one workable point in that space.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{W}{hether} speculatively prefetching pages speeds up or slows
down a process restored from a checkpoint hinges on a single variable:
round-trip time. We show that the same fixed prefetch policy
\emph{increases} time-to-first-request (TTFR) by $88\,\%$ at loopback RTT
yet \emph{reduces} it by $37\,\%$ once RTT exceeds $\approx\!125\,\mu$s; and,
counter-intuitively, the harmful case cuts the page-fault count by
$85\,\%$ even as latency worsens. This paper establishes that crossover for
CRIU lazy restore and gives a userspace runtime, \distriproc, that acts on
it.

Cloud platforms routinely checkpoint and migrate running
processes for load balancing, preemption recovery, and live
migration~\cite{clark2005live, sapuntzakis2002optimizing}. CRIU~\cite{criu} provides this
capability for Linux processes without application modification. Its
\emph{lazy restore} mode is particularly attractive: rather than waiting for
all memory pages to be copied before resuming the process, CRIU installs a
\uffd~\cite{userfaultfd, corbet2015userfaultfd} handler that fetches pages
on demand from a remote page server. The process begins running immediately,
and pages arrive as they are accessed.

The central operational question is what fetching policy the runtime should
use during this post-restore phase. Three options exist. \emph{Pure demand
paging} fetches only what the process explicitly faults on, contributing no
extra network traffic but potentially stalling on every new page.
\emph{Sequential prefetch} speculatively fetches nearby pages after each
fault, reducing future fault latency at the cost of additional network
traffic~\cite{chen1995effective}. An \emph{adaptive policy} monitors runtime
signals and switches between these behaviors based on observed conditions.

Prior deployments of CRIU lazy restore~\cite{criu-lazy, runc-lazy} have not
systematically studied the policy question; the literature has tended to
treat sequential prefetch as a uniformly beneficial default. The central
empirical finding of this paper is that prefetch is neither uniformly
beneficial nor uniformly harmful: its sign is set by round-trip time. At
loopback RTT, speculative prefetch competes with fault-path traffic over the
shared TCP channel, builds an async queue backlog, and delays synchronous
fault resolution, increasing TTFR by $88\,\%$ on large-working-set workloads
(Welch $t = -45.9$, $p < 10^{-9}$, $n = 20$), while \emph{reducing} page
faults by $85\,\%$, so the conventional proxy metric points opposite to the
observed latency. But this regime is bounded: injecting RTT with
\texttt{netem} reveals a crossover near $\approx\!125\,\mu$s RTT, above
which prefetch's latency-hiding dominates its congestion cost and prefetch
\emph{reduces} TTFR (by $37\,\%$ at $1$\,ms RTT). The paradox is thus a
property of the congestion-bound, near-zero-RTT regime, not of prefetch in
general.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Framing.}\, We position this work as a
\emph{measurement-driven systems study} of prefetch behavior in CRIU lazy
restore, supplemented by a practical mitigation. The controller we
present is a heuristic feedback loop in the tradition of TCP congestion
control and adaptive paging; we do not claim algorithmic novelty for it.
The novelty lies in (a)~the empirical characterization of the
fixed-prefetch TTFR paradox, which to our knowledge has not been reported
for CRIU lazy restore; (b)~the identification of an RTT crossover that
bounds the paradox and flips prefetch from harmful to beneficial; and
(c)~the demonstration that the harmful regime is mitigable in userspace
using only locally observable signals, without kernel changes or RDMA.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}\, This paper makes four contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{The RTT-dependent characterization (our central result).} At
loopback RTT, fixed sequential prefetch increases PyTorch TTFR by $88\,\%$
($650 \to 1{,}227$\,ms) while \emph{reducing} page faults by $85\,\%$, so
fault count is not a reliable proxy for latency. By injecting RTT with
\texttt{netem} we then locate a crossover near $\approx\!125\,\mu$s: below
it prefetch is harmful (congestion-bound), above it prefetch \emph{reduces}
TTFR (latency-bound, $-37\,\%$ at $1$\,ms; demand-only lazy even times out
at $2$\,ms). This maps \emph{when} speculative prefetch helps post-restore
(\S\ref{sec:paradox},~\S\ref{sec:crosshost}) and is confirmed on a real
two-machine LAN, where prefetch cuts TTFR by $72\,\%$ at $311\,\mu$s
(\S\ref{sec:twomachine}).

\item \emph{Generality across kernels and workloads.} The paradox and its
mitigation hold on both Linux $6.18.7$ and $7.0.9$ ($+88\,\%$ vs.\
$+37\,\%$; magnitude is kernel-dependent, \S\ref{sec:crosskernel}), and lazy
restore delivers a $24.3\times$ TTFR win for memory-light workloads
(\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\item \emph{A userspace mitigation.} A feedback controller
(duplicate-fetch pressure + queue depth, $128$-fault window) recovers
$572$\,ms of the congestion-bound regression, to within $5.5$\,ms of
demand-only lazy ($p = 0.42$, $n = 20$), using only locally observable
signals, no kernel changes or RDMA. Above the crossover the congestion
signals no longer apply cleanly and the controller behaves erratically,
motivating RTT-awareness (\S\ref{sec:crosshost}).

\item \emph{Reproducible artifact.} We release \distriproc as an
open-source userspace daemon (${\sim}1{,}200$ lines of C and
${\sim}300$ lines of Python) with deterministic benchmark scripts that
reproduce every result in this paper from a single command
(\S\ref{sec:repro}).
\end{itemize}

The baseline experiments use a TCP loopback page server; we then inject RTT
to characterize the network-latency axis directly (\S\ref{sec:crosshost}).
RDMA, writable remote-memory coherence, and multi-node distribution are out
of scope (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

% ── 2. Background ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{CRIU and Lazy Restore}

CRIU checkpoints a Linux process by dumping its memory pages, file
descriptors, and kernel state to an image on disk. \emph{Full restore}
copies all memory pages into the restored process before resuming
execution. This can take hundreds of milliseconds for processes with large
address spaces (\S\ref{sec:eval-ttfr}).

\emph{Lazy restore} defers page transfer. At restore time, CRIU marks the
process's virtual memory regions as unpopulated and registers a \uffd file
descriptor that intercepts page faults. When the process accesses an
unpopulated page, the kernel delivers a \texttt{UFFD\_EVENT\_PAGEFAULT}
event to the handler. The handler fetches the missing page from a remote
page server over TCP, installs it via \texttt{ioctl(UFFDIO\_COPY)}, and
returns control to the process. The process resumes execution at the
faulting instruction without any application-level change.

\subsection{Userfaultfd}

\texttt{userfaultfd(2)}~\cite{userfaultfd, corbet2015userfaultfd} is a Linux kernel
mechanism (available since kernel 4.3) that delegates page-fault handling
for a virtual memory range to userspace. The faulting thread is blocked
until the userspace handler installs a page via \texttt{UFFDIO\_COPY} (or
\texttt{UFFDIO\_ZEROPAGE}). \distriproc polls \uffd on the main thread,
resolves each fault synchronously over a primary TCP connection, and
dispatches prefetch work to a background thread over a secondary TCP
connection.

\subsection{The Post-Restore Remote-Memory Phase}

After lazy restore, the process operates in a \emph{remote-memory phase}:
some pages reside locally (those already faulted in) and the remainder
reside on the page server. This phase ends when all pages have been
accessed at least once. The duration depends entirely on the workload's
access pattern:

\begin{itemize}
\item A process that immediately touches all its memory (e.g., a model
server loading weights) faults on virtually every page before it can serve
its first request. Lazy restore exposes the full RTT of fetching each page.

\item A process that can serve requests using a small active footprint
(e.g., a loop server that touches only its stack) completes TTFR in
milliseconds, because only a handful of pages are needed before the first
response.
\end{itemize}

This distinction motivates workload-aware policy.

\textbf{Page server} (\texttt{criu\_page\_server.py}): a TCP server that
reads CRIU page image files and serves individual pages on request. It
accepts two connections per restore session: one for synchronous fault
resolution and one for async prefetch.

\textbf{Lazy handler} (\texttt{lazy\_handler.c}): the userspace daemon
that registers the restored process's memory regions with \uffd, then runs
a main loop that reads fault events, fetches pages synchronously over TCP,
and optionally queues prefetch work for a background thread.

\textbf{Hot-page profiler} (\texttt{hot\_pages.py}): an optional pass that
records the most-accessed pages before checkpointing and passes them to the
handler for eager prefetch at restore time. Not evaluated in this paper.

\subsection{Prefetch Modes}

The handler supports three policy modes, selectable at invocation time.

\textbf{Lazy (demand-only).}\, No speculative fetching. Each page fault is
resolved by fetching exactly the faulted page. Minimizes network traffic but
may stall on every unique page access.

\textbf{Fixed prefetch.}\, After each fault on page $P$, the handler queues
pages $P{+}1, \ldots, P{+}S$ (configurable stride $S$, window $W$) to the
background prefetch thread. Fetched over the secondary TCP connection. The
policy is fixed: it does not observe whether prefetch is beneficial.

\textbf{Adaptive prefetch.}\, Begins like fixed prefetch but activates a
controller that monitors signals within a sliding window of $N$ faults.
When the controller determines that prefetch is wasteful, it disables the
prefetch thread until conditions improve.

\subsection{Adaptive Controller}
\label{sec:controller}

The controller runs at the end of every $N$-fault window ($N\!=\!128$ in
all experiments). It observes three signals:

\textbf{Duplicate pressure} $\rho$ (\textit{dup\_rate}): the fraction of
prefetch requests within the current window that were for pages already
served. A high duplicate rate indicates the prefetch worker is re-requesting
pages the fault path already resolved.

\textbf{Queue depth} $q$ (\textit{qdepth}): the number of unprocessed
prefetch requests waiting in the bounded MPSC queue. A large queue
indicates outstanding prefetch traffic is occupying TCP bandwidth and
adding latency to the fault path.

\textbf{Queue drops}: prefetch requests dropped because the queue was full.
A nonzero count confirms the queue is saturated.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:controller} summarizes the controller, which applies a
two-tier rule against a bounded prefetch queue of $Q\!=\!8192$ entries.
The \emph{disable} tier fires when the duplicate rate reaches
$\dupthr\!=\!0.80$, the queue exceeds $Q/2$, or any request was dropped; a
milder \emph{halve} tier (duplicate rate $0.50$, or queue above $Q/4$)
shrinks $W$ and $S$ before resorting to a full disable; and prefetch
\emph{grows} again only when a window is clean (zero duplicates and queue
below $\drainthr\!=\!Q/32$). After a disable the controller observes a
$16$-window cooldown before a probe re-enable. These thresholds were set by
inspection of pilot runs and not tuned further; the controller is
intentionally simple (a first paper-worthy policy, not a final production
one), and \S\ref{sec:sensitivity} shows TTFR is insensitive to their exact
values.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Adaptive prefetch controller, run every $N$ faults}
\label{alg:controller}
\begin{algorithmic}
\Require window $N$; queue size $Q$; disable thresholds $\dupthr\!=\!0.80$,
         $Q/2$; halve thresholds $0.50$, $Q/4$; grow threshold
         $\drainthr\!=\!Q/32$; window $W$, stride $S$
\Ensure  prefetch enable flag and dynamic $W,S$
\Statex
\State $\rho \gets (\text{duplicate prefetches})/(\text{prefetch requests})$
\State $q \gets$ queue depth;\; $d \gets$ dropped requests
\If{in cooldown}
    \State decrement cooldown; on expiry re-enable with $W,S$ (probe)
\ElsIf{$\rho \ge \dupthr$ \textbf{or} $q > Q/2$ \textbf{or} $d > 0$}
    \State $W,S \gets 0$; disable prefetch; cooldown $\gets 16$ windows
\ElsIf{$\rho \ge 0.50$ \textbf{or} $q > Q/4$}
    \State $W \gets \lfloor W/2\rfloor$;\; $S \gets \lfloor S/2\rfloor$
           \Comment{disable if both reach $0$}
\ElsIf{$\rho = 0$ \textbf{and} $q < \drainthr$}
    \State $W \gets \min(W{+}2, W_{\max})$;\;
           $S \gets \min(S{+}1, S_{\max})$ \Comment{grow}
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Why not hit rate?}\, An intuitive signal would be the fraction of
prefetched pages subsequently faulted on. In practice this metric is
unreliable for async prefetch: if the worker installs a page before the
fault fires, no event registers the installation as a hit. In all our
experiments, the reported hit rate is $0\,\%$ despite active prefetch. The
controller therefore uses duplicate pressure and queue depth instead.

\subsection{Cost Model}
\label{sec:cost-model}

The controller's threshold choices follow from a simple analytical model of
the shared TCP channel. Let $r$ be the per-page round-trip time, $b$ the
effective per-connection throughput in pages per second, and $F$ the number
of unique pages the workload will fault on before TTFR. The expected TTFR
of demand-only lazy restore is then
\begin{equation}
  T_\textit{lazy} \;\approx\; F \, r,
  \label{eq:tlazy}
\end{equation}
bounded below by the network RTT and above by the bandwidth-delay product.
Sequential prefetch with stride $S$ issues $S$ additional requests per
fault. If a fraction $\delta$ of those requests are wasted (duplicates of
already-served pages), each fault still drives roughly
$(1{+}S(1{-}\delta))$ requests onto the shared channel, so the effective
service time per fault grows as the link saturates:
\begin{equation}
  T_\textit{prefetch} \;\approx\;
  F \,\bigl(\, r \;+\; \max\!\bigl(0,\; (1+S(1{-}\delta))/b \,-\, r \bigr) \,\bigr).
  \label{eq:tprefetch}
\end{equation}
Prefetch is therefore beneficial only while the second term stays at zero,
i.e.\ while the offered per-fault load remains under the link's per-request
budget:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1 + S\,(1{-}\delta)}{b} \;\le\; r.
  \label{eq:condition}
\end{equation}
The controller cannot measure $r$, $b$, or $\delta$ directly, but $q$ and
$\rho$ each capture one side of the trade-off. Queue depth $q$ tracks link
saturation: a growing queue is the direct signature of the second term in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:tprefetch} turning positive, so $\qthr\!=\!Q/2$ fires once the
offered load has begun to exceed the channel's drain rate ($\approx\!170$\,ms
of backlog at the measured page-server throughput). Duplicate pressure
$\rho$ captures the other side: a high $\rho$ means the sequential policy is
mispredicting the access order, so prefetch buys little latency reduction
even when it is cheap, and there is no upside left to pay for. Disabling on
either signal therefore covers both failure modes, prefetch that congests
($q$) and prefetch that does not pay off ($\rho$). We use the model to
\emph{explain} this boundary qualitatively, not to predict it: the thresholds
were set empirically, and because most mispredicted prefetches are suppressed
before transmission (\S\ref{sec:impl}), the steady-state offered-load term
\emph{under}-counts the transient fault-storm congestion that actually drives
the regression. The model bounds the direction of the effect, not its
magnitude.

% ── 4. Implementation ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:impl}

\subsection{Fault Path}

The main handler thread polls \uffd with a 1-second timeout. On each fault
event, it checks a shared hash set of already-served pages
(\S\ref{sec:sharedstate}). If served, it calls \texttt{ioctl(UFFDIO\_COPY)}
from local cache. Otherwise it sends a request over the primary TCP
connection, reads the page data, and installs it via \texttt{UFFDIO\_COPY}.
The faulting thread is unblocked as soon as \texttt{UFFDIO\_COPY} returns.

\subsection{Async Prefetch Path}

After resolving a fault on page $P$, the handler computes prefetch
candidates and attempts to push each to a bounded MPSC queue shared with
the prefetch thread. If the queue is full, the request is dropped and a
drop counter is incremented.

The prefetch thread dequeues candidates in order. For each candidate, it
checks the shared hash set. If the page is already served, it increments
the duplicate counter and discards the request. Otherwise it fetches the
page over the secondary TCP connection, installs it via
\texttt{UFFDIO\_COPY}, and marks it as served.

This architecture ensures the primary fault path is never blocked by
prefetch activity: the two TCP connections are independent, and the queue
bounds the memory footprint of outstanding requests.

\subsection{Shared State}
\label{sec:sharedstate}

The served-page set is a fixed-size open-addressing hash set
(\texttt{hashset.h}). Access is protected by a mutex. Both the fault path
(to avoid re-fetching pages already installed by the prefetch thread) and
the prefetch thread (to skip duplicate requests) consult it. Contention is
low because the prefetch thread operates at a slower pace than the fault
path on most workloads.

\subsection{Lifecycle Events}

The \uffd protocol generates non-fault events (\texttt{UFFD\_EVENT\_UNMAP},
\texttt{REMAP}, \texttt{REMOVE}, \texttt{FORK}) in addition to page faults.
\distriproc handles these explicitly: it logs \texttt{UNMAP} events and
exits cleanly on restore completion.

% ── 5. Evaluation ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Hardware:}\, AMD Ryzen 7 7735HS (8 cores), 15\,GB RAM,
x86\_64.\, \textbf{Software:}\, Linux 6.18.7, CRIU 4.2, Python 3.14, PyTorch
2.12 (CPU-only).\, \textbf{Transport:}\, TCP over the loopback interface
(127.0.0.1). All experiments run on a single machine; this represents
best-case network conditions.

\textbf{Methodology.}\, Each configuration runs $n\!=\!20$ iterations. We
report mean $\pm$ the $95\,\%$ confidence interval (CI) computed
from a Student's $t$-distribution with $n{-}1\!=\!19$ degrees of freedom
($t_{0.025,19}\!=\!2.093$). Pairwise comparisons use Welch's two-tailed

to a probe request.

\item \textbf{test\_loop}: a synthetic C process that increments a counter

\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Fixed-Prefetch Paradox}
\label{sec:paradox}

At loopback RTT, fixed prefetch on PyTorch reduces page faults by $85\,\%$
yet increases TTFR by $88\,\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:faults_vs_ttfr}); this is the
congestion-bound regime, and \S\ref{sec:crosshost} shows the effect reverses
once RTT exceeds $\approx\!125\,\mu$s. The paradox is explained by
TCP channel dynamics. The prefetch worker generates a backlog of pending
TCP reads on the secondary connection. Although this does not directly
block the primary connection, it congests the page server's socket send
buffer, delaying responses on the primary connection. The effect manifests
as growing queue depth $q$ and ultimately increases wall-clock time per
fault.

The $85\,\%$ fault reduction is real: far fewer faults fire. But each
surviving fault takes longer to resolve because its resolution is delayed by
congestion, and the net effect is higher TTFR. This is structurally analogous
to bufferbloat~\cite{gettys2011bufferbloat}: aggressive buffering masks
backpressure that the producer should have observed, allowing the queue to
grow to a point where latency dominates throughput gains. The same dynamic
motivates classical TCP congestion control~\cite{jacobson1988congestion}
and delay-sensitive variants such as TCP~Vegas~\cite{brakmo1995vegas},
which infer congestion from queueing delay rather than loss; our controller
applies the same idea at the prefetch-policy layer, using duplicate
pressure and queue depth as queue-delay proxies. A complementary kernel
signal that we do not yet consume is the Linux Pressure Stall Information
interface~\cite{linux-psi}, which exposes per-resource stall fractions and
could feed a future tighter feedback loop.

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

The thresholds $\dupthr\!=\!0.80$ and halve threshold $0.50$ were chosen
by inspection of pilot traces, raising the legitimate concern that the
controller is over-fit to its tuning data. Two observations push back.
First, the in-paper traces (Table~\ref{tab:controller}) show the duplicate
rate already at $97\,\%$ with $q\!=\!1454$ in the first PyTorch window, so
the \textsc{disable} branch fires under \emph{any} disable threshold
$\dupthr\!<\!0.97$; the Redis trace ($\mathit{dup}\!=\!98\,\%$,
$q\!=\!541$) disables under any $\dupthr\!<\!0.98$; \texttt{test\_loop}
never approaches the thresholds and correctly leaves prefetch enabled. The
duplicate-rate signal is either firmly above the largest plausible
threshold (memory-heavy) or firmly below the smallest (memory-light), so
the choice of $\dupthr$ within a wide middle range is largely cosmetic on
these workloads.
Second, we confirm this directly with a sweep over five disable/halve
pairs spanning aggressive ($30/10\,\%$) to permissive ($90/60\,\%$), each
run for $n\!=\!5$ on PyTorch via the harness
\texttt{eval/ablation\_thresholds.sh}, with the thresholds set through the
environment variables \texttt{DISTRIPROC\_DUP\_\{DISABLE,HALVE\}\_PCT}.

(Table~\ref{tab:ablation}): $628$--$637$\,ms, with every setting
statistically indistinguishable from the default
($|t|\!<\!1.4$, all $p\!>\!0.2$) and all within noise of demand-only lazy.
There is no sharp cliff: the controller is insensitive to the exact
threshold because the duplicate-rate signal is bimodal across our
workloads. (Because the regression itself is kernel-sensitive
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}), this sweep is only meaningful on the evaluation
kernel, Linux~$6.18.7$.)

\subsection{The RTT Crossover}
\label{sec:crosshost}

The results so far use a loopback page server, which is the best case for the
fault path and the worst case for prefetch: with near-zero RTT, demand-only
lazy pays almost nothing per fault, so speculative traffic can only add
congestion. To test whether the paradox survives real network latency, we
inject RTT on the page-transfer path with \texttt{tc netem} on the loopback
interface and re-run the PyTorch matrix at each setting ($n\!=\!10$). The
injection affects only IP traffic, so the CRIU control socket (AF\_UNIX) is
untouched and \texttt{full} restore (no page server) is an RTT-independent
reference. This is emulated latency on one host, but it
exercises the real TCP stack under non-trivial delay. It does not reproduce
cross-host bandwidth contention or NIC behaviour; we address that separately
with a real two-machine run (\S\ref{sec:twomachine}) that confirms the
above-crossover regime on physical hardware.

The effect of prefetch \emph{reverses} with RTT
(Fig.~\ref{fig:crosshost}, Table~\ref{tab:crosshost}). The loopback row of
this $n\!=\!10$ sweep ($626\!\to\!1{,}198$\,ms, $+91\,\%$) reproduces the
$n\!=\!20$ headline ($650\!\to\!1{,}227$\,ms, $+88\,\%$) within run-to-run
variation; the small offset is the smaller sample, not a different regime.
Fixed prefetch crosses from significantly harmful to significantly beneficial
between $100$ and $150\,\mu$s RTT: at $100\,\mu$s fixed ($2{,}455\!\pm\!72$\,ms,
$95\,\%$ CI) is still above lazy ($2{,}300\!\pm\!7$) with non-overlapping CIs,
while at $150\,\mu$s fixed ($2{,}919\!\pm\!59$) is below lazy
($3{,}126\!\pm\!19$), again non-overlapping. The crossover is therefore
bracketed by these two measurements, not interpolated from noise; we use its
${\approx}125\,\mu$s midpoint as shorthand:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Below the crossover (congestion-bound).} Demand-only lazy is
cheap per fault, and fixed prefetch is harmful: $+91\,\%$ at loopback,
$+42\,\%$ at $60\,\mu$s RTT. This is the regime the rest of the paper
studies, and the one the controller is designed for.

\item \textbf{Above the crossover (latency-bound).} Each demand fault now
pays a full RTT serially, so lazy degrades steeply ($16{,}700$\,ms at
$1$\,ms RTT; it fails to complete within our timeout at $2$\,ms). Fixed
prefetch hides this latency by overlapping transfers and \emph{wins}:
$-37\,\%$ vs.\ lazy at $1$\,ms RTT.
\end{itemize}

Below the crossover the adaptive controller tracks demand-only lazy
($640$ vs.\ $626$\,ms at loopback, $1{,}642$ vs.\ $1{,}649$ at $60\,\mu$s),
which is exactly right: there lazy is optimal and the controller's
duplicate/queue signals correctly disable the harmful prefetch. Above the
crossover its behaviour degrades, because those same congestion signals were
never designed for the latency-bound regime. Just below the crossover the
controller still helps: at $100\,\mu$s it disables the still-harmful prefetch
and is the fastest mode ($2{,}230\!\pm\!25$\,ms, below lazy
$2{,}300\!\pm\!7$ and fixed $2{,}455\!\pm\!72$). But at $150\,\mu$s, where
fixed has turned beneficial, the controller keeps disabling and tracks lazy
($3{,}208\!\pm\!74$ vs.\ lazy $3{,}126\!\pm\!19$; $95\,\%$ CIs overlap, so the
two are not separable) while both trail fixed ($2{,}919\!\pm\!59$): it forgoes
the win fixed now captures. Further out it happens to drift back toward
prefetch and recovers most of the benefit ($10{,}906$ vs.\ fixed $10{,}462$
at $1$\,ms, far below lazy $16{,}700$), but this is incidental rather than
intended. At $2$\,ms its congestion-driven disable misfires outright, leaving
it slower than simply keeping prefetch on ($16{,}409$ vs.\ $12{,}807$\,ms).
The controller's signals detect \emph{congestion} but not its
\emph{absence of alternative}: when serial demand paging is even slower,
wasteful-looking prefetch is the better choice, and a policy that disables on
congestion alone cannot see this. An RTT-aware policy that consults the
measured crossover before disabling is the natural next step
(\S\ref{sec:future}).

\subsection{Real Two-Machine Validation}
\label{sec:twomachine}

The RTT sweep above is emulated on one host. To confirm the above-crossover
regime on real hardware, we run the same PyTorch matrix with the page server
on a \emph{second physical machine} (a 4-core host, no GPU) reached over a
wired gigabit LAN, leaving the restore side unchanged on the primary host.
The measured round trip is $311\,\mu$s, above the crossover, so the model
predicts prefetch should win. It does, by a wider margin than the emulation
suggested (Table~\ref{tab:twomachine}, $n\!=\!50$): demand-only lazy averages
$7{,}073\!\pm\!124$\,ms while fixed prefetch averages $1{,}985\!\pm\!28$\,ms,
a $71.9\,\%$ reduction with non-overlapping $95\,\%$ CIs. The adaptive
controller tracks the winning prefetch side here ($2{,}133\!\pm\!30$\,ms,
$-69.8\,\%$ vs.\ lazy, within $7.5\,\%$ of fixed), rather than flapping as it
did near the emulated crossover (\S\ref{sec:crosshost}).

Two points follow. First, the crossover is not an artifact of \texttt{netem}:
the sign of the prefetch effect flips on genuine cross-host hardware exactly
as the emulation predicted. Second, the emulation was \emph{conservative}. At
a comparable $300\,\mu$s the \texttt{netem} sweep showed only a $16\,\%$
prefetch benefit (Table~\ref{tab:crosshost}), whereas the real LAN shows
$72\,\%$. The gap is real cross-host bandwidth contention, which delay
injection on loopback does not model: under that contention demand-only lazy
serializes a transfer per fault, while prefetch pipelines and hides it. The
single-host sweep therefore understates, not inflates, the benefit of
prefetch above the crossover.

% ── 6. Discussion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{When lazy restore helps and when it does not.}\, Lazy restore is
most beneficial for processes that can satisfy their first requests using
a small fraction of their address space. For test\_loop, TTFR is determined
by kernel restore overhead and a handful of pages, and lazy restore
eliminates the page-copy phase entirely ($24.3\times$ speedup). For PyTorch
inference, the process cannot serve its first request until all
$\approx 15{,}500$ model weight pages are local, and lazy restore imposes
a $3.4\times$ TTFR overhead relative to full restore. This workload
dependence is fundamental; we do not claim lazy restore universally reduces
TTFR.

\textbf{Adaptive recovers essentially all of the regression.}\, On PyTorch,
adaptive mode ($655$\,ms) sits just $5.5$\,ms above demand-only lazy
($650$\,ms), a gap that is not statistically significant
($t\!=\!-0.82$, $p\!=\!0.42$, $n\!=\!20$): the two are indistinguishable.
Because the controller disables prefetch in the very first control window
(Table~\ref{tab:controller}), only $128$ faults' worth of speculative
traffic is ever issued before back-off, so almost none of the
fixed-prefetch congestion is incurred. The conservative default threshold
is already aggressive enough to capture the recovery; the ablation
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity}) shows tightening it further yields no measurable
gain.

\textbf{Duplicate pressure as an adaptive signal.}\, Duplicate pressure is
effective because it directly measures wasted work without requiring
knowledge of the workload's access pattern. A high duplicate rate means the
prefetch worker is fetching pages the fault path has already resolved:
the workload is accessing memory in an order the sequential policy did not
predict. Queue depth amplifies this signal: even moderate duplicate
pressure becomes harmful when the queue is large, because queued-but-
useless requests occupy TCP bandwidth. On both memory-heavy workloads the
combination saturates within the first $128$-fault window and the
controller disables immediately (PyTorch: $97\,\%$ duplicate, $q\!=\!1454$;
Redis: $98\,\%$, $q\!=\!541$).

\textbf{Deployment guidance.}\, For operators considering CRIU lazy restore
in production, our measurements suggest four operational rules. First,
check the transport RTT against the crossover (\S\ref{sec:crosshost}): above
$\approx\!125\,\mu$s, which covers most WAN and cross-host paths, prefetch
hides latency and should stay \emph{enabled}. Many intra-datacenter and
same-rack paths ($5$--$50\,\mu$s) and RDMA fabrics ($1$--$2\,\mu$s) sit
\emph{below} the crossover, however, so the adaptive controller and the rest
of this guidance target that sub-crossover (loopback / same-host /
RDMA-class) regime where prefetch can congest. Second, in that regime estimate the
working-set ratio (touched pages before TTFR over total mapped pages); if it
is below ${\sim}5\,\%$, demand-only lazy already suffices. Third, size the
bounded prefetch queue $Q$ from the page-server's sustained throughput $b$
so that the disable threshold $\qthr\!=\!Q/2$ corresponds to a tolerable
queueing delay $\Delta_\textit{tolerable}\!\approx\!(Q/2)/b$; with our
$Q\!=\!8192$ and $b\!\approx\!24{,}000$\,pages/s this caps added delay near
$\Delta_\textit{tolerable}\!\approx\!170$\,ms. Fourth, separate the
fault-path and prefetch-path TCP connections at the page-server end
(distinct sockets, ideally distinct kernel send buffers) so the disable
decision can drain prefetch backlog without simultaneously stalling fault
responses; collapsing them onto one socket removed nearly all of the
adaptive controller's benefit in our pilot runs.

% ── 7. Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Checkpoint/restore and live migration.}\, Process-level
checkpoint/restore predates CRIU~\cite{litzkow1997checkpoint,
sapuntzakis2002optimizing}. VM-level live
migration~\cite{clark2005live} popularized the pre-copy and post-copy
techniques~\cite{hines2009postcopy} that CRIU~\cite{criu} now inherits;
adjacent memory-management work in this lineage includes ballooning and
share-based reclamation in commodity hypervisors~\cite{waldspurger2002memory}.
Lazy restore via \uffd~\cite{criu-lazy} is structurally a post-copy technique
specialized for processes. Fast in-memory CRIU for containers has been
explored~\cite{venkatesh2019fast}, but without an adaptive prefetch policy;
existing CRIU deployments default to demand-only fetching.

\textbf{Serverless cold start.}\, A closely related body of work targets
sub-second function startup. Catalyzer~\cite{du2020catalyzer} reaches
sub-millisecond cold start via initialization-less booting;
REAP~\cite{ustiugov2021reap} accelerates Firecracker microVM~\cite{agache2020firecracker}
snapshots by replaying the working set on restore.
Mohan~\textit{et~al.}~\cite{mohan2019agile} characterize agile cold-start
techniques at scale, and Nightcore~\cite{shen2021nightcore} targets
latency-sensitive serverless microservices. Production studies of serverless
cold-start behavior~\cite{wang2018peeking, shahrad2020serverless} report TTFR
distributions that mirror the regime we evaluate. \distriproc differs in
that it focuses on the \emph{post-restore} TCP-coupled prefetch policy rather
than the snapshot or boot path itself.

\textbf{Remote and disaggregated memory.}\,
Infiniswap~\cite{gu2017infiniswap}, Fastswap~\cite{amaro2020can}, and
Leap~\cite{al2020leap} implement transparent remote memory over RDMA by
replacing kernel swap with remote page fetch; Leap in particular learns
prefetch patterns at swap-path granularity, which is structurally similar
to the signal-driven policy we adopt at the \uffd boundary.
Google's software-defined far memory at warehouse
scale~\cite{lagar2019software} pioneered tiered remote-memory deployment
in production. AIFM~\cite{ruan2020aifm}, Carbink~\cite{zhou2022carbink},
and Hydra~\cite{lee2022hydra} extend RDMA-backed remote memory with
runtime APIs and fault tolerance. Semeru~\cite{wang2020semeru} and
Clio~\cite{calciu2021clio} co-design managed runtimes and hardware for
disaggregated memory. LegoOS~\cite{shan2018legoos} disaggregates CPU,
memory, and storage across separate components, and CXL-based memory
pooling~\cite{cxl} is an emerging hardware approach; the network
requirements of resource disaggregation are characterized
in~\cite{gao2016network}, and earlier blade-server proposals
include~\cite{lim2009disaggregated}. These systems operate at the swap or
application level; \distriproc targets the transient remote-memory state
that exists between restore and working-set convergence, and is
complementary: its userspace policy layer could sit above a CXL-backed
or RDMA-backed far-memory transport.

\textbf{Prefetch policies.}\, Sequential and stride prefetching in virtual
memory systems are well-studied~\cite{chen1995effective}. Adaptive
prefetch has been explored in hardware
prefetchers~\cite{srinath2007feedback, joseph1997prediction} and database
buffer management~\cite{cao1996integrated}, and machine-learning models
can in principle predict access patterns directly~\cite{hashemi2018learning}.
To our knowledge, adaptive prefetch policy for the \uffd post-restore
phase has not been previously studied.

\textbf{Container live migration.}\, RunC and Podman support CRIU-based
container migration~\cite{runc-lazy}. Page server implementations are
typically stateless TCP servers similar to ours. No published work
evaluates adaptive fetch policy for container migration TTFR.

% ── 9. Limitations ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

We organize the threats to validity as follows. \emph{Construct validity}:
the PyTorch throughput measurement does not capture the remote-memory phase
(below). \emph{Internal validity}: at $n\!=\!20$ the headline effects are
resolved with wide margins, but very small differences (e.g.\ the $5.5$\,ms
adaptive-vs-lazy gap) remain below our resolution. \emph{External validity}:
loopback transport, a single host, a single CPU architecture, and, most
consequentially, a single kernel version constrain how far the results
generalize. Each is treated in turn.

\textbf{Transport and RTT.}\, The baseline page server is TCP loopback;
\S\ref{sec:crosshost} then injects RTT with \texttt{netem} to characterize
the latency axis directly, locating the crossover near $\approx\!125\,\mu$s
where prefetch flips from harmful to beneficial. The emulated sweep is
confirmed on real hardware by a two-machine run at $311\,\mu$s
(\S\ref{sec:twomachine}), which reproduces the above-crossover regime and
shows the emulation was conservative. Two caveats remain. The emulated points
capture added latency but not cross-host bandwidth contention, and the real
two-machine measurement is a single RTT operating point rather than a full
physical sweep across the crossover. And we did not evaluate RDMA, whose
$\approx\!1$--$2\,\mu$s RTT sits deep in the congestion-bound regime where the
controller applies; confirming this on RDMA hardware is future work.

\textbf{Controller is not RTT-aware.}\, The controller disables prefetch on
congestion signals alone, which is correct below the crossover but ill-suited
above it: there its behaviour is erratic, worst-of-both near the crossover
($150\,\mu$s) and, at $2$\,ms RTT, slower than simply leaving prefetch on
(\S\ref{sec:crosshost}). The fix, gating the disable decision on a measured
RTT estimate relative to the crossover, is sketched in \S\ref{sec:future} but
not implemented or evaluated here.

\textbf{Writable coherence.}\, \distriproc does not implement write-back or
write-through to the page server. Pages installed via
\texttt{UFFDIO\_COPY} are local copies; modifications are not propagated.
The system supports read-only remote memory only.

\textbf{Controller generalization.}\, Thresholds were set by inspection
on three workloads and were not cross-validated against a separate
holdout. A workload with a mixed access pattern (sequential bursts mixed
with random reads) may behave unpredictably. The controller is a
heuristic; it is not learned.

\textbf{PyTorch throughput measurement.}\, Throughput is measured after
all pages are local. It does not capture inference throughput during the
remote-memory phase.

\textbf{Single-machine baseline.}\, The loopback and \texttt{netem} matrices
run page server and process on one host. The two-machine run
(\S\ref{sec:twomachine}) exercises a real cross-host path at one RTT; a full
physical RTT sweep and a true live-migration deployment remain future work.

\textbf{Kernel sensitivity.}\, The headline numbers are from Linux~$6.18.7$;
the absolute TTFR and the regression \emph{magnitude} are kernel-dependent
(\S\ref{sec:crosskernel}). On stock Linux~$7.0.9$ the lazy baseline is
$\approx\!3\times$ higher and the fixed-prefetch regression is $+37\,\%$
rather than $+88\,\%$, but the paradox and the controller's recovery both
persist and remain statistically significant. We hypothesise that the
magnitude shift stems from $7.0$-series changes in the mechanisms involved:
the reworked swap-in path (the swap-table ``unified swapin'' work), batched
large-folio fault/reclaim handling, and Accurate ECN (RFC~9768) enabled by
default. We have not isolated which dominates, and a full sweep across kernel
versions is future work.

\textbf{Sample size.}\, We run $n\!=\!20$ iterations per configuration.
The headline effects ($88\,\%$ TTFR change for fixed prefetch on PyTorch)
yield $p\!<\!10^{-9}$ with tight confidence intervals, and the
adaptive-vs-lazy comparison is firmly non-significant ($p\!=\!0.42$). The
residual $5.5$\,ms mean gap is below the resolution of this sample and we
do not claim it is real. We have not characterized tail behavior (e.g.\
$p99$ TTFR), which would need substantially more iterations.

% ── 10. Future Work ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Several directions follow naturally from the present work.

\textbf{RTT-aware controller.}\, The most direct follow-up is to make the
disable decision RTT-aware. \S\ref{sec:crosshost} shows a crossover near
$\approx\!125\,\mu$s below which prefetch should be throttled and above
which it should be encouraged. The handler can estimate RTT cheaply from
its own fault round-trips and gate the congestion-driven disable on whether
the estimate sits below the crossover, so the policy converges to lazy on
loopback and to fixed prefetch on a real network, capturing the prefetch
benefit the current congestion-only controller forfeits above the crossover.

\textbf{RDMA transport.}\, RDMA's $\approx\!1$--$2\,\mu$s RTT sits deep in
the congestion-bound regime, so we expect the paradox, and the value of the
controller, to persist there; confirming this on RDMA hardware, where the
per-fault cost model changes, is open.

\textbf{Learned thresholds.}\, $\dupthr$ and $\qthr$ are currently fixed.
A per-workload calibration pass during the first few hundred faults could
fit them from observed $\rho$ and $q$ distributions, and prior work on
learning memory access patterns~\cite{hashemi2018learning} suggests a
classifier could replace the duplicate-pressure heuristic entirely with
modest online cost.

\textbf{Multi-tenant page servers.}\, Production deployments will see
several restored processes attached to one page server; whether the
controller's per-connection signals remain accurate when the bandwidth
budget is split across tenants is an open question. A first step would be
to share a $q$-budget across connections and let the controller arbitrate
per-tenant.

\textbf{Writable coherence.}\, Read-only remote memory limits applicability
to checkpoint-then-resume workflows. Adding a write-back path with a
coarse coherence mode would broaden the scope but introduces new tradeoffs
(when to push dirty pages, how to avoid amplifying the same congestion
problem on the outbound path) that deserve dedicated study.

\textbf{Cross-architecture validation.}\, All measurements use an x86\_64
host with $4$\,KiB pages. ARM64 servers commonly use $16$\,KiB or
$64$\,KiB base pages, changing both $F$ and $b$ in the cost model
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:tprefetch}) by an order of magnitude. Whether the
controller's heuristic thresholds transfer unchanged or need
per-page-size calibration is an empirical question we leave open.

\textbf{Integration with kernel memory pressure.}\, The Linux PSI
interface~\cite{linux-psi} exposes \texttt{some} and \texttt{full} stall
fractions per memory cgroup at sub-second granularity. A tighter feedback
loop could read PSI directly to corroborate the queue-depth signal,
particularly to distinguish prefetch-induced contention from genuine
working-set pressure during the post-restore phase. Wiring PSI into the
controller is a small implementation task; characterising whether it
adds signal beyond $\rho$ and $q$ is the open question.

% ── 11. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a measurement study of speculative prefetch in the CRIU
lazy-restore post-restore phase, and \distriproc, a userspace runtime that
acts on it. Our central finding is that whether fixed sequential prefetch
helps or hurts is governed by round-trip time: at loopback RTT it increases
memory-heavy TTFR by $88\,\%$ ($n\!=\!20$) by congesting the shared
fault-path channel, while cutting page faults $85\,\%$, so fault reduction
misleads, but above a crossover near $\approx\!125\,\mu$s RTT it instead
reduces TTFR (by $37\,\%$ at $1$\,ms), because demand-only lazy must
serialize a full RTT per fault. A simple per-window controller using
duplicate pressure and queue depth detects the congestion-bound regime and
disables prefetch, recovering essentially all of the loopback regression
(within $5.5$\,ms of demand-only lazy, $p\!=\!0.42$) and cutting prefetch
volume $52$--$55\,\%$ on memory-light workloads; above the crossover the same
congestion signals no longer fit and its behaviour is erratic, motivating the
RTT-aware policy we sketch as future work. The
system is implemented in userspace without kernel modifications and every
result is reproducible from a single benchmark invocation.

\section{Derivation of the Cost-Model Thresholds}
\label{app:cost}

We sketch the derivation behind the threshold choices in
\S\ref{sec:cost-model}. Let the page server sustain $b$ pages/s on a single
TCP connection, with mean per-page round trip $r$. The fault path issues
one request per unique page fault and waits for the response inline; the
prefetch path adds, per fault, $S$ speculative requests of which a fraction
$\delta$ are wasted (the page is already served). With fault rate
$\lambda_f$, the total request rate offered to the shared channel is
\begin{equation}
  \lambda \;=\; \lambda_f \cdot \bigl(1 + S(1-\delta)\bigr),
  \label{eq:offered}
\end{equation}
and the link's utilization is
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\!\textit{link}} \;=\; \frac{\lambda}{b}.
  \label{eq:rho-link}
\end{equation}
Modeling the channel as an $M\!/\!M\!/1$ queue with arrival rate $\lambda$
and service rate $b$, mean queueing wait obeys
\begin{equation}
  W \;=\; \frac{\rho_{\!\textit{link}}}{b\,(1-\rho_{\!\textit{link}})},
  \label{eq:meandelay}
\end{equation}
which diverges as $\rho_{\!\textit{link}}\!\to\!1$. By Little's law the
expected backlog observed at the prefetch MPSC queue is
\begin{equation}
  q \;=\; \lambda \, W,
  \label{eq:little}
\end{equation}
so the controller's measured $q$ is a direct, scale-free proxy for
$\rho_{\!\textit{link}}$ through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:meandelay}--\eqref{eq:little}:
$q$ remains small while $\rho_{\!\textit{link}}$ is comfortably below $1$
and explodes once it nears saturation. While $\lambda < b$ the queue stays
bounded and the channel is in its underloaded regime; the prefetch
contribution sits entirely in the link's slack. Once $\lambda$ approaches
$b$, the per-fault service time grows as $1/(b-\lambda)$, after which any
additional speculative load is pure harm. The controller's two thresholds
bracket this transition: $\dupthr$ caps $\delta$ at the point where the
predicted waste exceeds the unsupplied slack of the link, and $\qthr$ caps
$q$ at the point where Eq.~\eqref{eq:little} already implies
$\rho_{\!\textit{link}}\!\gtrsim\!1$ regardless of the estimate of $\delta$.
The grow threshold $\drainthr\!=\!Q/32$ is set far below the disable
threshold $\qthr\!=\!Q/2$ so prefetch only ramps back up once the queue is
nearly empty and cannot immediately re-saturate the channel. The same shape would arise for any policy in which
speculative work shares a bandwidth-limited transport with synchronous
work; this is why the same parameters work, qualitatively, across our
three workloads despite their very different access patterns.

\section{Welch's Test and Confidence Intervals}
\label{app:stats}

We restate the statistical machinery used to produce Tables~\ref{tab:ttfr}
and~\ref{tab:ttest}, for completeness. Let $X_1,\dots,X_{n_1}$ and
$Y_1,\dots,Y_{n_2}$ be independent TTFR samples from two restore modes
with sample means $\bar{X},\bar{Y}$ and sample variances $s_1^2,s_2^2$.
Welch's two-sample $t$-statistic, which does not assume equal variances,
is
\begin{equation}
  t \;=\;
  \frac{\bar{X} - \bar{Y}}
       {\sqrt{\,s_1^2/n_1 \,+\, s_2^2/n_2\,}}.
  \label{eq:welch-t}
\end{equation}
The associated effective degrees of freedom are approximated by the
Welch--Satterthwaite formula
\begin{equation}
  \nu \;\approx\;
  \frac{\bigl(s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2\bigr)^{2}}
       {\dfrac{(s_1^2/n_1)^{2}}{n_1-1} \,+\, \dfrac{(s_2^2/n_2)^{2}}{n_2-1}},
  \label{eq:welch-df}
\end{equation}
which is what produces the non-integer $\textit{df}$ values reported in
Table~\ref{tab:ttest} (e.g.\ $\textit{df}\!=\!33.8$ for the
full~vs.~lazy comparison on PyTorch).
The $95\,\%$ confidence intervals in Table~\ref{tab:ttfr} are the standard
$t$-distribution intervals,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CI}_{95\%} \;=\;
  \bar{X} \;\pm\; t_{0.025,\,n-1}\,\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}},
  \label{eq:ci}
\end{equation}
with $n\!=\!20$ and $t_{0.025,19}\!=\!2.093$. For $p$-values we use a
two-tailed test against the null $\mathbb{E}[X]\!=\!\mathbb{E}[Y]$. Even at
$n_1\!=\!n_2\!=\!20$ a sufficiently small mean difference stays below the
resolution of the test: the $5.5$\,ms adaptive--lazy gap on PyTorch yields
$t\!=\!-0.82$, $p\!=\!0.42$ (Table~\ref{tab:ttest}), so we treat adaptive
and demand-only lazy as statistically indistinguishable rather than claiming
the residual gap is real (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Worked Numerical Example on PyTorch}
\label{app:worked}

This worked example is \emph{descriptive}: we did not derive the controller's
behavior from the model in advance and then confirm it. We observed the
regime experimentally, then instantiate
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:tlazy}--\eqref{eq:condition} to check how far a simple
shared-channel model accounts for what we measured: it matches the
demand-only branch closely and the fixed-prefetch branch only in direction.
With that caveat, we use the measured PyTorch numbers. Profiling the lazy (demand-only) run gives $F\!\approx\!15{,}515$
unique pages and $b\!\approx\!24{,}000$~pages/s sustained on the secondary
TCP connection, with measured per-page round trip $r\!\approx\!42\,\mu$s
on loopback. Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:tlazy},
\begin{equation}
  T_\textit{lazy} \;\approx\; 15{,}515 \times 42\,\mu\text{s} \;\approx\; 652\,\text{ms},
  \label{eq:tlazy-num}
\end{equation}
which is within $1\,\%$ of the measured mean $650$\,ms reported in
Table~\ref{tab:ttfr}, confirming the demand-only branch of the model is
quantitatively sound. The fixed-prefetch branch is where the simple model
stops being predictive. With $S\!=\!8$ the duplicate rate reaches $97\,\%$
within the first window (Table~\ref{tab:controller}), so most speculative
requests are suppressed at the served-set check before they reach the wire
(\S\ref{sec:impl}); the steady-state offered-load term
$(1\!+\!S(1{-}\delta))/b$ is correspondingly small and \emph{under}-predicts
the measured $1{,}227$\,ms. The extra $577$\,ms is instead a transient:
during the opening fault storm, before duplicate suppression catches up, the
prefetch worker contends with the fault path for the page server's send path
and inflates per-fault service time. The model captures the \emph{direction}
of this effect (speculative work sharing a bandwidth-limited channel becomes
harmful as utilization rises) but not its magnitude, which is why the
controller is driven by the measured $\rho$ and $q$ rather than by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:tprefetch}. The adaptive controller disables prefetch in that
first window, before the storm-phase congestion accumulates, recovering the
$T_\textit{lazy}$ regime to within $5.5$\,ms.
